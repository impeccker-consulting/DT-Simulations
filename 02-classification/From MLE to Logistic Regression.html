Machine Learning for Data-Driven Decision Making

The Evidence Room

Every logistic regression is really a detective story: you have some evidence (your data), a suspect list of possible parameters, and a rule for picking the parameter that makes the evidence most believable. That rule is called Maximum Likelihood Estimation, or MLE.

This module builds that idea from scratch — no calculus required — using sliders, live numbers, and one running business example: will a student pass, based on hours studied? The same logic applies to "will a customer buy" or "will a borrower default."

Step 01

Why a straight line breaks on Yes/No questions

Suppose you want to predict whether a customer buys (1) or doesn't (0), based on how many minutes they spent on a product page. Ordinary regression fits a straight line through 0s and 1s — and that line keeps going, forever, in both directions.

Bought (1) Didn't buy (0) Straight-line fit (OLS)
The break: A straight line can predict a "probability" of buying that is −18% or 140%. Probabilities can only live between 0% and 100% — a straight line simply doesn't know that rule.
The fix, in one sentence: instead of fitting a straight line to 0/1 outcomes, we fit a bendy, S-shaped curve that is mathematically forced to stay between 0 and 1 — and we use MLE to choose which S-curve fits best. Steps 2–5 build exactly that, piece by piece.
Step 02

You don't know the salesperson's true closing probability p — but you can guess a value of p, and ask: "how likely was it that I'd see exactly this outcome, if p were really this?" That question, asked and answered for every possible p, is the entire idea of Maximum Likelihood.

Business reading: the p that makes the observed 10 pitches most believable is simply the actual success rate you saw. MLE just formalises "believe the data" — and this exact machinery is what will soon estimate regression coefficients instead of a single p.
Step 03

From probability, to odds, to log-odds

Probability is capped between 0 and 1, which is exactly why a straight line can't model it directly. But if we re-express probability as odds, and then take the log of the odds, we get a number that can freely run from −∞ to +∞ — perfectly suited to a straight line. This log-odds is called the logit, and it is what logistic regression actually fits a straight line to, behind the scenes.

Odds = p / (1−p)

The top scale is probability (boxed 0→1). The bottom scale is the logit (unbounded). Drag the slider and watch the marker travel freely on the logit line, even as it's squeezed on the probability line.
Odds, familiar from betting: "3 to 1 odds" means 3 successes for every 1 failure, i.e. p = 0.75. Odds = p ÷ (1−p).
Logit = ln(odds). At p = 0.5, odds = 1, logit = 0 — the natural "50–50" midpoint. As p → 1, logit → +∞. As p → 0, logit → −∞.
The one-line bridge: logistic regression assumes logit(p) = β₀ + β₁x — an ordinary straight line, just measured on the logit scale instead of the probability scale. Converting that line back to a probability is exactly what Step 04 visualises.
Step 04

The S-curve simulator

Un-doing the logit gives the famous sigmoid function: p(x) = 1 / (1 + e−(β₀+β₁x)). It looks intimidating written out, but it does one simple job: it takes any straight line and folds it into an S-shape that can never leave the 0–1 corridor. Drag the two sliders below and watch exactly what each coefficient controls.

Step 06

Carrying this into a business decision

Swap "hours studied" for "marketing spend," "pass/fail" for "buy/don't buy," and "student" for "customer" or "loan applicant" — the machinery is identical. Here is the whole module compressed into three ideas.

01 — Why not a straight line

Yes/No outcomes need predictions trapped between 0 and 1. A straight line can't promise that; an S-curve can.

02 — What MLE actually does

It searches across possible coefficients and keeps the ones that make the data you actually observed the most believable — measured by log-likelihood.

03 — Reading the output

β₁ > 0 means the factor raises the odds of "Yes"; β₁ < 0 lowers it. eβ₁ tells you the odds-multiplier per unit increase — the number you'd actually quote in a business review.

One-paragraph recap: Logistic regression models the log-odds of an outcome as a straight line in your predictors, then converts that line into a bounded probability with the sigmoid function. Since there's no simple formula to "solve" for the best-fitting line the way ordinary regression has, we instead search for it — trying coefficient after coefficient and keeping the ones under which the observed Yes/No data was most likely to occur. That search process is Maximum Likelihood Estimation.
